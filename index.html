01 Electronics Fundamentals

Ohm's Law & Power DEFINITELY ASKED

▶
V = I × R   |   P = V × I   |   P = I²R   |   P = V²/R

V = Voltage (Volts), I = Current (Amperes), R = Resistance (Ohms), P = Power (Watts)

Quick examples they might ask

A 100Ω resistor has 5V across it. What current flows?
I = V/R = 5/100 = 0.05A = 50mA
What power does a 10Ω resistor dissipate with 2A through it?
P = I²R = 4 × 10 = 40W

Kirchhoff's Laws (KCL & KVL)

  • KCL — Sum of currents entering a node = sum leaving. Current is conserved.
  • KVL — Sum of all voltages around a closed loop = 0. Voltage is conserved.

Passive Components DEFINITELY ASKED

▶
Resistor
Limits current. Unit: Ohm (Ω). Colour code: BBROYGBVGW (0–9)
Capacitor
Stores charge. Unit: Farad (F). Blocks DC, passes AC. Used for filtering, decoupling.
Inductor
Stores energy in magnetic field. Unit: Henry (H). Passes DC, blocks AC.
Diode
Allows current one way only. Forward voltage ~0.7V (silicon). Used for rectification, protection.

Series vs Parallel

Series R: R_total = R1 + R2 + R3
Parallel R: 1/R_total = 1/R1 + 1/R2 (two resistors: R1×R2 / R1+R2)
Series C: 1/C_total = 1/C1 + 1/C2  |  Parallel C: C_total = C1 + C2

Capacitor behaviour to remember

  • At DC steady state: capacitor is an open circuit
  • At very high frequency: capacitor is a short circuit
  • Decoupling caps (100nF) placed near IC power pins to suppress noise
  • Electrolytic caps are polarised — reversed polarity causes failure/explosion

Transistors — BJT & MOSFET LIKELY ASKED

▶

BJT (Bipolar Junction Transistor)

  • Pins: Base, Collector, Emitter
  • NPN: current flows Collector → Emitter when base is driven HIGH
  • PNP: current flows Emitter → Collector when base is driven LOW
  • Current-controlled device. Used as amplifier or switch.
  • VBE ≈ 0.7V to turn on a silicon BJT

MOSFET

  • Pins: Gate, Drain, Source
  • N-channel: turns ON when Gate is HIGH (voltage-controlled)
  • P-channel: turns ON when Gate is LOW
  • Used in power electronics, motor drives, switching power supplies
  • Very high input impedance — gate draws almost no current

Power Supply Concepts DEFINITELY ASKED

▶
LDO (Linear Dropout)
Simple regulator. Converts higher V to lower V. Wastes excess as heat. E.g., 5V → 3.3V.
Buck Converter
Switching regulator. Steps voltage DOWN efficiently. Used in most modern boards.
Boost Converter
Switching regulator. Steps voltage UP. E.g., 3.3V battery → 5V output.
Ripple
AC noise on a DC supply. Measured in mV. Caused by switching frequency. Filtered by caps.

Common failure symptoms from power issues

  • Board not powering on → check input voltage, fuse, LDO output
  • IC getting hot → overcurrent, wrong voltage, reversed polarity, shorted output
  • Erratic resets → power rail dropping under load (check with scope, not just DMM)
  • Voltage correct but device not working → check ground continuity
Key tip: Always measure voltage at the IC pin, not just at the connector. Voltage drop across traces can matter.
02 Lab Instruments

Digital Multimeter (DMM) DEFINITELY ASKED

▶

Modes and what they measure

DC Voltage (V—)
Measure battery voltage, rail voltages, logic levels. Probes: red to positive, black to GND.
AC Voltage (V~)
Measure mains supply, AC signals. Do NOT use on DC circuits expecting AC.
Resistance (Ω)
Power OFF the circuit. Measure component value or trace resistance.
Continuity (beep)
Beeps if resistance < ~30Ω. Used for checking traces, solder joints, shorts.
Diode test
Shows forward voltage drop. Silicon ~0.6–0.7V. Open circuit in reverse = good diode.
DC Current (A)
Break circuit, insert meter IN SERIES. Use correct current port on DMM.

How to answer: "Walk me through checking a dead board with a DMM"

Your answer
First I check input voltage at the power connector — confirm the right voltage is arriving. Then I measure the output of the power regulator (LDO or buck converter) to see if the rail is up. Then I check continuity on the ground plane. Then I measure voltage at the VCC pin of the main IC. If all rails are correct but the device is dead, I move to checking communication lines or looking for heat signatures manually.

Oscilloscope DEFINITELY ASKED

▶

Key parameters

  • Timebase (X axis) — time per division. Adjust to see your signal period clearly.
  • Voltage scale (Y axis) — volts per division. Adjust so signal fills ~60% of screen.
  • Trigger — stabilises the waveform on screen. Set trigger level to ~50% of signal amplitude.
  • Probe compensation — square wave test before use. Adjust probe trim cap until square is clean.
  • Ground clip — always connect probe ground to circuit ground.

What you use a scope for in FA work

  • Checking power rail noise/ripple (DMM shows average, scope shows transients)
  • Verifying clock signals — frequency, duty cycle, clean edges
  • Checking SPI/I2C/UART communication waveforms
  • Measuring PWM duty cycle
  • Catching glitches and spikes that cause erratic behaviour
Scope shows what DMM cannot: transient dips, noise spikes, signal integrity issues. If DMM shows correct voltage but device behaves erratically — grab the scope.

Power Analyser & Spectrum Analyser LIKELY ASKED

▶

Power Analyser

  • Measures voltage, current, power simultaneously with high accuracy
  • Can show power factor, efficiency, harmonics
  • Used for testing power supplies, motor drives, chargers
  • Different from DMM — DMM measures point-in-time; power analyser logs over time

Spectrum Analyser

  • Shows frequency content of a signal (X = frequency, Y = amplitude)
  • Used to check RF emissions, noise floor, clock harmonics
  • Important in consumer electronics for EMI/EMC compliance testing
  • At Foxconn: checking for spurious emissions from phone or device PCBAs
If asked "have you used a spectrum analyser?"
I haven't used one directly, but I understand it shows the frequency domain representation of a signal. In NPI/FA context, it's used to identify EMI issues and validate RF performance of products. I'm familiar with the concept and ready to learn hands-on.
03 Hardware Debugging

Debugging Methodology DEFINITELY ASKED

▶

The systematic approach — say this in your interview

  1. Define the symptom clearly — what exactly is failing? Is it reproducible?
  2. Check power first — always. All rails correct? Ground connected?
  3. Visual inspection — burnt components, cold solder joints, missing parts, polarity errors
  4. Isolate the fault domain — power section? Communication? Firmware? Mechanical?
  5. Divide and conquer — split the circuit in half, test each half. Narrow it down.
  6. Measure, don't assume — probe it. Don't guess.
  7. Document findings — every measurement, every test, every result.
  8. Root cause, not just symptom fix — if cap was reversed, why? Design issue? Assembly error?

Common failure modes to know

Cold solder joint
Dull, grainy appearance. High resistance. Intermittent contact. Common SMD failure.
Solder bridge
Solder connecting two pins that should be separate. Causes shorts. Visible under microscope.
Open trace
Broken PCB trace. Continuity test shows open. Caused by mechanical stress, corrosion.
ESD damage
Electrostatic discharge kills ICs silently. Device may work then fail. Requires IC replacement.
Reversed polarity
Capacitor or diode placed backwards. Causes overheating, bulging, circuit failure.
Wrong component
Similar package, wrong value or part number. Visual and electrical test both needed.

Communication Protocols in Debugging LIKELY ASKED

▶
UART
2 wires: TX, RX. No clock. Async. Speed set by baud rate. Most common for debug output.
I2C
2 wires: SDA, SCL. Addressed. Multiple devices on one bus. Pull-up resistors required.
SPI
4 wires: MOSI, MISO, SCK, CS. Fast. Full duplex. One master, multiple slaves via CS.
GPIO
General Purpose I/O. Digital high/low. Can be input or output. Used for control signals.

Debugging comms with a scope

  • UART: Check baud rate matches. Look for clean transitions. Voltage levels correct (3.3V or 5V logic)?
  • I2C: Check pull-up resistors present. SCL clock running? ACK bit visible?
  • SPI: CS going LOW before data? Clock edge correct (CPOL/CPHA)?
04 Failure Analysis Techniques

FA Flow — NPI Context DEFINITELY ASKED

▶

In NPI FA at Foxconn, your job is to find why a unit failed — not just that it failed. This drives corrective action so the problem doesn't repeat in production.

Standard FA flow

  1. Visual inspection — naked eye, magnifier, microscope. Look for obvious damage.
  2. Electrical characterisation — DMM and scope measurements. Reproduce the failure.
  3. Fault isolation — narrow down to component, joint, or area.
  4. Physical analysis — cross-section, SEM/EDS, X-ray as needed.
  5. Root cause identification — design? material? process? assembly?
  6. Corrective action — 8D report, FMEA update, design change.

SEM / EDS / X-ray / Cross-section LIKELY ASKED

▶
SEM (Scanning Electron Microscope)
Very high magnification imaging. Shows surface morphology, crack details, fracture surfaces. Much more detail than optical microscope.
EDS (Energy Dispersive Spectroscopy)
Used WITH SEM. Identifies chemical elements at a point or area. "What material is this?" Confirms contamination, wrong alloy, corrosion products.
X-ray inspection
Non-destructive. Views solder joints under BGA/QFN without removing component. Detects voids, bridges, missing balls. Standard in PCBA FA.
Cross-sectional analysis
Cut and polish the sample. View solder joint internal structure under microscope. Confirms voiding, intermetallic layer thickness, crack propagation.
If asked "have you used SEM/EDS?"
I haven't operated SEM/EDS directly. However, I understand their role in FA — SEM gives high-magnification surface imaging while EDS identifies elemental composition, which together help confirm contamination, wrong materials, or corrosion as root causes. I'm ready to work with lab partners or learn in-house equipment.
05 DOE, 8D & FMEA

8D Problem Solving DEFINITELY ASKED

▶

8D = 8 Disciplines. Standard corrective action framework used in manufacturing. Foxconn uses this heavily.

  1. D1 — Team: Form a cross-functional team
  2. D2 — Problem description: Define the problem with data (5W2H: Who, What, Where, When, Why, How, How many)
  3. D3 — Containment action: Stop the bleeding immediately. Quarantine affected units.
  4. D4 — Root cause: Use 5-Why, Fishbone (Ishikawa) diagram. Find the TRUE root cause.
  5. D5 — Corrective action: Choose and verify the fix.
  6. D6 — Implement: Roll out corrective action permanently.
  7. D7 — Prevent recurrence: Update FMEA, work instructions, control plans.
  8. D8 — Congratulate team: Close the report, recognise contributions.
Most important to remember: D3 (containment) and D4 (root cause). They will ask "what's the difference between containment and corrective action?"
What's the difference between containment and corrective action?
Containment is an immediate temporary fix to prevent further damage — like quarantining suspect units. Corrective action is the permanent fix that addresses the root cause so the failure never happens again.

FMEA (Failure Mode & Effects Analysis) DEFINITELY ASKED

▶

FMEA is a proactive risk analysis tool. Done BEFORE failures happen, to predict and prevent them.

Key columns in an FMEA table

  • Failure Mode — What could go wrong? (e.g., capacitor open circuit)
  • Effect — What happens if it does? (e.g., power supply fails, device dead)
  • Severity (S) — How bad is the effect? Rated 1–10.
  • Occurrence (O) — How often might it happen? Rated 1–10.
  • Detection (D) — How likely is it to be caught before reaching customer? Rated 1–10.
  • RPN (Risk Priority Number) — S × O × D. Higher = more critical. Prioritise highest RPNs.
RPN = Severity × Occurrence × Detection

Goal: reduce RPN by improving detection, reducing occurrence, or reducing severity.

DOE (Design of Experiments) LIKELY ASKED

▶

DOE is a structured method to find which factors affect an output, and by how much.

Simple example to explain in interview

If solder joint strength varies, DOE would systematically test combinations of: reflow temperature, solder paste volume, and pad size — to find which factor matters most and what the optimal setting is.

Key concepts

  • Factor — An input variable you control (temperature, speed, pressure)
  • Level — The values a factor takes (low/high, or specific values)
  • Response — The output you're measuring (yield, strength, resistance)
  • Full factorial — Test every combination. Accurate but slow if many factors.
  • Taguchi method — Orthogonal arrays. Fewer experiments, still finds main effects.
Simple interview answer
DOE helps us systematically vary process parameters to understand which ones truly affect the output. Instead of changing one thing at a time (which is slow and misses interactions), DOE tests combinations in a structured way and uses statistical analysis to find the key drivers.

5-Why Analysis LIKELY ASKED

▶

Ask "Why?" repeatedly until you reach the root cause. Usually takes 5 iterations.

Example — board not powering on

  • Why is the board dead? → LDO output is 0V
  • Why is LDO output 0V? → Input capacitor is shorted
  • Why is capacitor shorted? → It was placed with reversed polarity
  • Why was it reversed? → Component orientation marking unclear in assembly drawing
  • Why was the drawing unclear? → No polarity indicator in silkscreen
  • Root cause: PCB silkscreen lacked polarity marking → fix the design and update assembly instructions
06 Sensors — ALS & Proximity

Ambient Light Sensor (ALS) LIKELY ASKED

▶

ALS measures the intensity of ambient light in lux. Used in smartphones to auto-adjust screen brightness.

How it works

  • Contains a photodiode that generates current proportional to light intensity
  • ADC converts the current/voltage to a digital value
  • Output in lux. Interface: usually I2C
  • Spectral response tuned to match human eye (peaks around 550nm green)

Common ICs

BH1750 APDS-9930 OPT3001 TSL2561 VEML7700

FA issues with ALS

  • Covered by protective film during testing → reads 0 lux incorrectly
  • Wrong I2C address configuration
  • Integration time not set correctly → saturated or too-slow readings
  • Physical damage to lens/window above sensor

Proximity Sensor LIKELY ASKED

▶

Detects if an object is near without physical contact. In smartphones: turns off screen when held to ear.

How it works

  • IR LED emits infrared light pulses
  • Photodetector measures reflected IR from nearby object
  • More reflection = object closer
  • Often combined with ALS in same IC package
  • Interface: I2C, with interrupt output for threshold crossing

Common ICs

APDS-9930 VCNL4010 RPR-0521RS LTR-559

FA issues with proximity sensor

  • IR LED not driving (open circuit or wrong current limiting resistor)
  • Crosstalk — IR reflecting off device housing, not actual object
  • Threshold registers set incorrectly → triggers too early or not at all
  • Black protective tape over sensor window (assembly error)
07 Analog & Digital Circuits

Analog vs Digital DEFINITELY ASKED

▶
Analog signal
Continuous range of values. E.g., microphone output, temperature sensor voltage, audio signal.
Digital signal
Only two states: HIGH (1) or LOW (0). E.g., UART TX line, GPIO output, SPI clock.
ADC
Analog to Digital Converter. Converts real-world signals to numbers. Resolution in bits (12-bit ADC = 4096 levels).
DAC
Digital to Analog Converter. Converts numbers to analog voltage. Used in audio output, signal generation.

Logic families and voltage levels

  • 5V TTL logic: HIGH > 2V, LOW < 0.8V
  • 3.3V CMOS logic: HIGH > 2V, LOW < 0.8V (approximately)
  • Level shifting: Needed when 5V device talks to 3.3V device. Direct connection can damage 3.3V IC.

Op-Amp basics

  • Amplifies the difference between two inputs (inverting and non-inverting)
  • Used as: voltage amplifier, comparator, filter, buffer
  • Comparator mode: output goes HIGH or LOW depending on which input is larger
  • Virtual ground rule: in feedback configuration, both inputs are at same voltage

Digital Logic Gates GOOD TO KNOW

▶
AND
Output HIGH only if ALL inputs HIGH. Both A AND B must be 1.
OR
Output HIGH if ANY input HIGH. A OR B is 1.
NOT (Inverter)
Output is opposite of input. 0→1, 1→0.
NAND
AND + NOT. Output LOW only if all inputs HIGH. Universal gate.
XOR
Output HIGH if inputs are DIFFERENT. Used in error detection, addition circuits.
Flip-flop (D-FF)
Stores one bit. Output changes on clock edge. Foundation of registers and memory.

09 PCB & EDA

Reading Schematics DEFINITELY ASKED

▶

Key things to read in a schematic

  • Power rails — VCC, GND, 3V3, 5V. Follow the power flow first.
  • Reference designators — R1 = resistor 1, C4 = capacitor 4, U2 = IC 2, Q1 = transistor
  • Net labels — Same label = connected, even if no wire drawn between them
  • Decoupling caps — 100nF caps near IC VCC pins. Filter power noise.
  • Pull-up/pull-down resistors — On I2C lines, reset pins, enable pins
  • Test points — TP1, TP2 etc. Dedicated pads for probing. Your DMM/scope targets.

PCB layers (2-layer board)

  • Top layer — components and signal traces
  • Bottom layer — usually ground plane or more traces
  • Silkscreen — component labels, polarity marks, test point labels
  • Solder mask — the green (or other color) coating. Protects traces.
Your strength here
You have actually designed 2-layer PCBs. Mention this. "I've designed 2-layer PCBs in KiCad, so I'm comfortable reading schematics and understanding how layout relates to electrical performance. I understand how decoupling caps are placed, how ground planes work, and how to read net labels."

You list FreeRTOS — explain what it is and how you've used it.
FreeRTOS is a real-time operating system for microcontrollers. It allows you to run multiple tasks concurrently — for example, one task handles sensor reading while another handles BLE communication. Key concepts are tasks (like threads), queues (for inter-task data passing), semaphores (for synchronisation), and task priorities. I used FreeRTOS on ESP32 and nRF52 projects to separate concerns — sensor polling in one task, communication in another — so the system remains responsive.
Difference between I2C and SPI — when would you choose one over the other?
I2C uses 2 wires (SDA, SCL) and supports multiple devices with addressing — good when you have many sensors and limited pins. SPI uses 4 wires (MOSI, MISO, SCK, CS) per device but is faster and full-duplex — better when you need high data throughput, like displays or ADCs. I choose I2C for slow sensors like temperature or light, SPI for fast peripherals like flash memory or high-speed ADCs.

It's much faster than guessing — you see exactly what's happening on the wire.

14 General Interview Questions — All Types

Technical Rapid-Fire Questions DEFINITELY ASKED

▶
What is the difference between a microcontroller and a microprocessor?
A microcontroller (MCU) has CPU, RAM, flash, and peripherals all on one chip — self-contained, used in embedded systems. A microprocessor is just the CPU — needs external RAM, storage, and peripherals. ESP32 and nRF52 are microcontrollers. A Raspberry Pi uses a microprocessor (ARM Cortex-A) and requires an OS.
What is a pull-up resistor and where do you use it?
A pull-up resistor connects a signal line to VCC so the default state is HIGH when nothing is driving it. I2C lines (SDA, SCL) require pull-ups — typically 4.7kΩ — because the bus uses open-drain drivers that can only pull the line low. Without pull-ups, the line floats and communication fails.
What is PWM and where is it used?
Pulse Width Modulation — a digital signal switched rapidly between HIGH and LOW. The duty cycle (% time HIGH) controls the effective power. Used for: LED brightness control, motor speed control, servo position control, DAC approximation. A 50% duty cycle at 5V behaves like 2.5V average to a slow-responding load.
What is ESD and how do you protect against it?
Electrostatic discharge — a sudden transfer of static electricity that can permanently damage ICs. Protection: TVS diodes or ESD protection diodes on exposed pins (USB, connectors, GPIOs). Design-level: ground planes, proper PCB layout, ESD protection ICs. In the lab: anti-static wrist strap, ESD mat, storing ICs in anti-static bags.
What is the difference between volatile and non-volatile memory?
Volatile memory loses data when power is removed — RAM (SRAM, DRAM). Non-volatile retains data without power — Flash, EEPROM, ROM. In embedded systems: program code lives in Flash (non-volatile), variables live in SRAM (volatile). Data you want to survive a reset (settings, calibration) must be stored in Flash or EEPROM.
What is a watchdog timer?
A hardware timer that resets the MCU if firmware doesn't "kick" (reset) it within a set time window. It's a safety mechanism — if firmware hangs or enters an infinite loop, the watchdog fires and reboots the system. Used in production embedded systems to ensure reliability without human intervention.
What is the purpose of a decoupling capacitor?
Placed close to IC power pins (between VCC and GND). Acts as a local charge reservoir. When the IC switches rapidly and demands a burst of current, the decoupling cap supplies it instantly — preventing the power rail from drooping and causing noise on other parts of the circuit. Typical values: 100nF ceramic close to IC, 10µF bulk cap nearby.
What is the difference between RS232 and RS485?
RS232 is single-ended — voltage relative to ground, short distance (~15m), point-to-point. RS485 is differential — uses two wires (A and B), detects the difference between them, much more noise-immune, up to 1200m, supports multiple devices on one bus. Modbus RTU (used in my PZEM library) runs over RS485.
What causes a ground loop and how do you fix it?
A ground loop occurs when two parts of a circuit have different ground potentials — usually when there are multiple ground paths with different impedances. Causes noise, hum in audio, measurement errors. Fix: use a single ground reference point (star grounding), use differential measurement, or use optoisolators/transformers to break the loop.
A component is getting very hot. What do you check first?
First: is the voltage correct? Overvoltage causes excess power dissipation. Second: is the current within rating? Overcurrent = P = I²R → excess heat. Third: is the component the right part (check BOM vs actual part number)? Fourth: is polarity correct for capacitors/diodes? Fifth: is the thermal design adequate — does it need a heatsink or better airflow?

NPI & FA Specific Questions DEFINITELY ASKED

▶
What is NPI and what does an NPI engineer do?
NPI — New Product Introduction — is the process of taking a product from design to mass production. An NPI engineer bridges the gap: validating that the design works at scale, identifying failures during pilot builds, driving corrective actions, and ensuring the production process is stable before full ramp. In FA, the NPI engineer investigates why units fail during this phase and feeds that learning back into the design and process.
What is yield and how do you improve it?
Yield = percentage of units that pass all tests. If 90 out of 100 boards pass, yield is 90%. To improve yield: identify the most common failure modes (Pareto analysis), find their root causes via FA, implement corrective actions (design change, process change, better incoming quality control), then verify yield improves. FMEA helps predict and prevent yield issues before they happen.
What is a Pareto chart and how is it used in FA?
A Pareto chart ranks failure modes from most frequent to least — bar chart ordered by count, with cumulative percentage line. The "80/20 rule" usually applies: 80% of failures come from 20% of causes. In FA, you focus corrective action on the top 2–3 failure modes first — fixing those gives the biggest yield improvement for the least effort.
What is ICT vs FCT?
ICT (In-Circuit Test) — tests the PCBA electrically before it's assembled into a product. Checks component values, shorts, opens using a bed-of-nails fixture. Fast, catches assembly defects. FCT (Functional Test) — tests the fully assembled product under real operating conditions. Verifies it works as intended. ICT catches assembly errors; FCT catches functional failures.
What would you do if you found a failure that was happening randomly and couldn't be reproduced?
Intermittent failures are the hardest. My approach: first stress the unit — temperature cycling, vibration, power cycling — to make it fail more frequently. Second, look for marginal signals with a scope that DMM would miss. Third, check solder joints under magnification — intermittent contact from cold joints is a common cause. Fourth, check for ESD sensitivity. Fifth, monitor power rails under dynamic load — voltage droop under current spikes. Document every condition when failure occurs — patterns reveal the trigger.
How do you write a failure analysis report?
A good FA report covers: unit identification and failure description, test conditions when failure occurred, measurements taken (with data/photos), fault isolation steps taken, root cause identified, evidence supporting the root cause (photos, waveforms, EDS data), corrective action recommended, and recurrence prevention steps. It should be clear enough that someone who wasn't in the lab can understand what happened and why.
