01 Electronics Fundamentals
✓

Ohm's Law & Power DEFINITELY ASKED

▶
V = I × R  |  P = V × I  |  P = I²R  |  P = V²/R

V = Voltage (Volts), I = Current (Amperes), R = Resistance (Ohms), P = Power (Watts)

Quick examples they might ask

A 100Ω resistor has 5V across it. What current flows?
I = V/R = 5/100 = 0.05A = 50mA
What power does a 10Ω resistor dissipate with 2A through it?
P = I²R = 4 × 10 = 40W

Kirchhoff's Laws

  • KCL — Sum of currents entering a node = sum leaving. Current is conserved.
  • KVL — Sum of all voltages around a closed loop = 0. Voltage is conserved.

Passive Components DEFINITELY ASKED

▶
Resistor
Limits current. Unit: Ohm (Ω). Colour code: BBROYGBVGW (0–9)
Capacitor
Stores charge. Unit: Farad (F). Blocks DC, passes AC. Used for filtering, decoupling.
Inductor
Stores energy in magnetic field. Unit: Henry (H). Passes DC, blocks AC.
Diode
Allows current one way only. Forward voltage ~0.7V (silicon). Used for rectification, protection.

Series vs Parallel

Series R: R_total = R1 + R2 + R3
Parallel R: R = (R1 × R2) / (R1 + R2)
Series C: 1/C_total = 1/C1 + 1/C2  |  Parallel C: C_total = C1 + C2

Capacitor behaviour to remember

  • At DC steady state: capacitor is an open circuit
  • At very high frequency: capacitor is a short circuit
  • Electrolytic caps are polarised — reversed polarity causes failure
  • Decoupling caps (100nF) placed near IC power pins to suppress noise

Transistors — BJT & MOSFET LIKELY ASKED

▶

BJT (Bipolar Junction Transistor)

  • Pins: Base, Collector, Emitter
  • NPN: current flows Collector → Emitter when base is driven HIGH
  • PNP: current flows Emitter → Collector when base is driven LOW
  • Current-controlled device. VBE ≈ 0.7V to turn on.

MOSFET

  • Pins: Gate, Drain, Source
  • N-channel: turns ON when Gate is HIGH (voltage-controlled)
  • P-channel: turns ON when Gate is LOW
  • Very high input impedance — gate draws almost no current

Power Supply Concepts DEFINITELY ASKED

▶
LDO
Linear Dropout Regulator. Simple, wastes excess voltage as heat. E.g., 5V → 3.3V.
Buck Converter
Switching. Steps voltage DOWN efficiently. Used in modern boards.
Boost Converter
Switching. Steps voltage UP. E.g., 3.3V → 5V.
Ripple
AC noise on DC supply. Measured in mV. Filtered by caps.

Common failure symptoms from power issues

  • Board not powering on → check input voltage, fuse, LDO output
  • IC getting hot → overcurrent, wrong voltage, reversed polarity
  • Erratic resets → power rail dropping under load (check with scope)
  • Voltage correct but device dead → check ground continuity
Key tip: Always measure voltage at the IC pin, not just at the connector.

02 Lab Instruments
✓

Digital Multimeter (DMM) DEFINITELY ASKED

▶
DC Voltage (V—)
Measure battery, rail voltages, logic levels. Red to positive, black to GND.
AC Voltage (V~)
Measure mains supply. Do NOT use on DC circuits.
Resistance (Ω)
Power OFF the circuit first. Measure component value or trace resistance.
Continuity (beep)
Beeps if resistance <~30Ω. Check traces, solder joints, shorts.
Diode test
Shows forward voltage drop. Silicon ~0.6–0.7V. Open in reverse = good diode.
DC Current (A)
Break circuit, insert meter IN SERIES. Use correct current port on DMM.

How to answer: "Walk me through checking a dead board with a DMM"

Your answer
Check input voltage at power connector → measure LDO/buck output → check ground continuity → measure VCC at the main IC pin → if rails correct but device dead, check communication lines or thermal issues.

Oscilloscope DEFINITELY ASKED

▶
  • Timebase (X axis) — time per division. Adjust to see your signal period clearly.
  • Voltage scale (Y axis) — volts per division. Fill ~60% of screen.
  • Trigger — stabilises waveform. Set to ~50% of signal amplitude.
  • Probe ground clip — always connect to circuit ground first.

Scope vs DMM — when to use which

Scope shows what DMM cannot: transient dips, noise spikes, signal integrity issues. If DMM shows correct voltage but device behaves erratically — grab the scope.
  • Use DMM for: steady voltage levels, continuity, resistance, slow measurements
  • Use scope for: power rail noise/ripple, clock signals, SPI/I2C/UART waveforms, PWM, glitches

Power Analyser & Spectrum Analyser LIKELY ASKED

▶

Power Analyser

  • Measures voltage, current, power simultaneously with high accuracy over time
  • Shows power factor, efficiency, harmonics — unlike DMM which is point-in-time

Spectrum Analyser

03 Hardware Debugging
✓

Debugging Methodology DEFINITELY ASKED

▶

The systematic approach — say this in your interview

  1. Define the symptom clearly — what exactly is failing? Reproducible?
  2. Check power first — always. All rails correct? Ground connected?
  3. Visual inspection — burnt components, cold solder joints, polarity errors
  4. Isolate the fault domain — power? comms? firmware? mechanical?
  5. Divide and conquer — split the circuit, test each half, narrow down
  6. Measure, don't assume — probe it. Every time.
  7. Document findings — every measurement, every test result
  8. Root cause, not symptom fix — WHY did it fail?

Common failure modes

Cold solder joint
Dull, grainy. High resistance. Intermittent contact.
Solder bridge
Connects two pins that should be separate. Short circuit.
Open trace
Broken PCB track. Continuity test shows open.
ESD damage
Electrostatic discharge kills ICs silently. Requires replacement.
Reversed polarity
Cap or diode backwards. Overheating, bulging, failure.
Wrong component
Similar package, wrong value. Visual + electrical test needed.

✓

Communication Protocols in Debugging LIKELY ASKED

▶
UART
2 wires: TX, RX. No clock. Async. Speed set by baud rate. Main debug output.
I2C
2 wires: SDA, SCL. Addressed. Multiple devices one bus. Pull-ups required.
SPI
4 wires: MOSI, MISO, SCK, CS. Fast. Full duplex. CS per slave.
GPIO
Digital high/low. Can be input or output. Used for control signals.

Debugging comms with a scope or logic analyser

  • UART: Check baud rate matches. Voltage levels correct (3.3V or 5V)?
  • I2C: Pull-up resistors present? SCL clock running? ACK bit visible?
  • SPI: CS going LOW before data? Clock edge correct (CPOL/CPHA)?

05 DOE, 8D & FMEA
✓

8D Problem Solving DEFINITELY ASKED

▶
  1. D1 — Team: Form a cross-functional team
  2. D2 — Problem description: Define with data (5W2H)
  3. D3 — Containment action: Stop the bleeding. Quarantine affected units.
  4. D4 — Root cause: Use 5-Why, Fishbone/Ishikawa diagram
  5. D5 — Corrective action: Choose and verify the fix
  6. D6 — Implement: Roll out corrective action permanently
  7. D7 — Prevent recurrence: Update FMEA, work instructions, control plans
  8. D8 — Congratulate team: Close the report
Most important: D3 (containment) and D4 (root cause). They WILL ask the difference.
What's the difference between containment and corrective action?
Containment is an immediate temporary fix to prevent further damage — like quarantining suspect units. Corrective action is the permanent fix that addresses the root cause so the failure never happens again.

FMEA — Failure Mode & Effects Analysis DEFINITELY ASKED

▶
  • Failure Mode — What could go wrong?
  • Effect — What happens if it does?
  • Severity (S) — How bad is the effect? 1–10.
  • Occurrence (O) — How often might it happen? 1–10.
  • Detection (D) — Likely to be caught before customer? 1–10.
  • RPN — Risk Priority Number. Higher = more critical.
RPN = Severity × Occurrence × Detection (max = 1000)

Goal: reduce RPN. Focus on highest RPNs first.

DOE — Design of Experiments LIKELY ASKED

▶
  • Factor — An input variable you control (temperature, speed, pressure)
  • Level — The values a factor takes (low/high)
  • Response — The output you measure (yield, strength)
  • Full factorial — Test every combination. Accurate but slow.
  • Taguchi method — Orthogonal arrays. Fewer experiments, finds main effects.
Simple interview answer
DOE helps us systematically vary process parameters to understand which ones truly affect the output — instead of changing one thing at a time (slow and misses interactions), DOE tests structured combinations and uses statistics to find key drivers.

5-Why Analysis LIKELY ASKED

▶

Example — board not powering on

  • Why dead? → LDO output is 0V
  • Why 0V? → Input capacitor shorted
  • Why shorted? → Reversed polarity placement
  • Why reversed? → Component orientation unclear in assembly drawing
  • Why unclear? → No polarity indicator in PCB silkscreen

Proximity Sensor LIKELY ASKED

▶

Detects object proximity without contact. In phones: turns off screen when held to ear.

  • IR LED emits pulses → photodetector measures reflected IR
  • More reflection = object closer
  • Often combined with ALS in same IC package
  • Interface: I2C with interrupt output for threshold crossing

07 Analog & Digital Circuits
✓

Analog vs Digital & ADC/DAC DEFINITELY ASKED

▶
Analog signal
Continuous range. E.g., microphone, temperature sensor voltage, audio.
Digital signal
Only HIGH or LOW. E.g., UART TX, GPIO, SPI clock.
ADC
Analog → Digital. Resolution in bits. 12-bit = 4096 levels over Vref.
DAC
Digital → Analog. Numbers to voltage. Used in audio output.
ADC resolution = Vref / 2^n  |  12-bit, 3.3V: 3.3/4096 ≈ 0.8mV per step

Logic voltage levels

  • 5V TTL: HIGH > 2V, LOW < 0.8V
  • 3.3V CMOS: HIGH > 2V, LOW < 0.8V
  • Level shifting needed when 5V device connects to 3.3V device — direct connection can damage the 3.3V GPIO

Logic Gates & Op-Amp Basics GOOD TO KNOW

▶
AND
Output HIGH only if ALL inputs HIGH.
OR
Output HIGH if ANY input HIGH.
NAND
AND + NOT. Universal gate.
XOR
HIGH if inputs DIFFERENT. Used in error detection.

Op-Amp

  • Amplifies difference between inverting and non-inverting inputs
  • Used as: amplifier, comparator, filter, buffer
  • Comparator mode: output goes HIGH or LOW based on which input is larger

14 General Interview Questions
✓

Technical Rapid-Fire Questions DEFINITELY ASKED

▶
Microcontroller vs microprocessor?
MCU: CPU + RAM + flash + peripherals on one chip. Self-contained for embedded. Microprocessor: just the CPU, needs external RAM and storage. ESP32/nRF52 = MCU. Raspberry Pi = microprocessor.
What is a pull-up resistor?
Connects a signal line to VCC so default state is HIGH. I2C SDA and SCL require pull-ups (~4.7kΩ) because the bus uses open-drain drivers — without pull-ups, the line floats and communication fails.
What is PWM and where is it used?
Pulse Width Modulation — digital signal rapidly switching HIGH/LOW. Duty cycle controls effective power. Used for LED brightness, motor speed, servo position control.
What is ESD and how do you protect against it?
Electrostatic discharge — sudden static electricity transfer that damages ICs. Protection: TVS diodes on exposed pins, ground planes, anti-static wrist strap in lab, ESD bags for storage.
Volatile vs non-volatile memory?
Volatile loses data when power off — RAM. Non-volatile retains — Flash, EEPROM. Embedded: code in Flash, variables in SRAM. Settings/calibration that must survive reset go in Flash or EEPROM.
What is a watchdog timer?
Hardware timer that resets the MCU if firmware doesn't "kick" it within a set window. Safety mechanism — if firmware hangs, watchdog fires and reboots. Used in production for reliability.
What is a decoupling capacitor?
Placed between VCC and GND near IC power pins. Supplies burst current when IC switches rapidly — prevents power rail drooping. Typical: 100nF ceramic close to IC, 10µF bulk cap nearby.
RS232 vs RS485?
RS232: single-ended, short distance (~15m), point-to-point. RS485: differential (A and B wires), noise-immune, up to 1200m, supports multiple devices. Modbus RTU (my PZEM library) runs over RS485.
A component is getting very hot. What do you check first?
Voltage correct? Current within rating? Right component (check BOM)? Polarity correct? Adequate thermal design? Check in this order.

NPI & FA Specific Questions DEFINITELY ASKED

▶
What is NPI and what does an NPI engineer do?
New Product Introduction — taking a product from design to mass production. NPI engineer bridges the gap: validates design works at scale, investigates failures during pilot builds, drives corrective actions, ensures production process is stable before full ramp.
What is yield and how do you improve it?
Yield = % of units passing all tests. Improve by: Pareto analysis of failure modes, FA on top failures, corrective action (design/process change), verify yield improvement. FMEA predicts and prevents yield issues.
ICT vs FCT?
ICT (In-Circuit Test): tests PCBA electrically before assembly into product. Checks components, shorts, opens using bed-of-nails fixture. Catches assembly defects. FCT (Functional Test): tests fully assembled product under real conditions. Catches functional failures.
How do you write a failure analysis report?
Unit ID and failure description → test conditions → measurements taken with data/photos → fault isolation steps → root cause with evidence → corrective action recommended → recurrence prevention. Clear enough that someone not in the lab can understand what happened and why.

Twisted Theory Questions WATCH OUT

▶
Same concepts you know — asked from a different angle to catch you if you memorised without understanding.
A capacitor is fully charged. How much current flows through it?
Zero. Fully charged capacitor in DC = open circuit. Current only flows while charging or discharging.
You measure 3.3V on a GPIO pin with a DMM. Does that mean the pin is outputting correctly?
Not necessarily. DMM has very high impedance — barely loads the pin. Under real load the pin might collapse. Measure under actual operating conditions or use a scope.
Scope shows clean 3.3V SPI clock. But sensor doesn't respond. What next?
Clock is fine — check: (1) CS going LOW before clock? (2) MOSI data correct? (3) SPI mode (CPOL/CPHA) correct for this sensor? (4) Clock frequency within sensor's max? (5) Sensor VCC — check at IC pin, not just supply.
Two identical boards, same batch — one works, one doesn't. What does that tell you?
Design is probably fine. Failure is likely assembly defect or component variation. Compare boards physically — use working board as reference, measure same test points on both, find where readings diverge. That's your fault location.
Board works at room temp but fails after 30 minutes. What do you suspect?
Thermal failure. Suspects: electrolytic cap losing capacitance with heat, solder joint that opens with thermal expansion, component near thermal limit, LDO going into thermal shutdown. Use thermal camera to find the hot component.
You replace a capacitor and the board works. Is your job done?
No. That fixes the symptom. Ask: why did this cap fail? Wrong voltage rating? Reversed? Counterfeit? Thermal overstress? Answer determines risk to all other boards in the batch. Document, check remaining stock, initiate corrective action.
DMM beeps continuity between VCC and GND. Always a problem?
Not always. Many circuits have LEDs, low-value resistors, or forward-biased diodes between VCC and GND. Check the schematic. A true short shows near-zero Ω and a component getting hot. Always measure the resistance value, don't just react to the beep.
You increase I2C pull-up from 4.7kΩ to 47kΩ. What happens?
Communication becomes slower and unreliable. Higher pull-up means slower rise time (RC constant increases). At 400kHz Fast mode, line may not reach valid HIGH before next clock edge — device stops responding or gives corrupted data. Higher I2C speed → lower pull-up resistance value.
5V sensor connected directly to 3.3V MCU GPIO. Sensor outputs 5V high. Risk?
Risk: damage to MCU — most 3.3V GPIO pins are not 5V-tolerant. 5V on 3.3V input can permanently damage it. Fix: voltage divider (10kΩ + 20kΩ), or level shifter IC, or Zener clamp to 3.3V. Always check datasheet — some GPIOs are marked 5V-tolerant.

Real-World Scenario Questions DEFINITELY ASKED

▶
You're given 50 failed units and 4 hours. Walk me through what you do.
Triage first — power on each, group by symptom. Pick the largest group (one root cause = most units fixed). Visual inspection across the group. Electrical: check power rails on 5–10 units, find where voltage diverges. Isolate to component or area. Document every measurement. After 4 hours: leading hypothesis, supporting evidence, recommended next step.
Customer says phone screen turns off randomly when held to ear. What's the likely cause?
Proximity sensor issue. Investigate: (1) threshold registers too sensitive? (2) IR crosstalk from device housing? (3) ALS interference in bright light? (4) Protective film or adhesive over sensor window from assembly? (5) Reproduce on multiple units — single defect or systemic?
New PCB revision comes in. Old revision worked. New one doesn't power on. First move?
Get the ECN — what exactly changed? Focus debugging on changed areas first. Check new component datasheets for different pin assignments or enable logic. Power up old revision alongside new, measure same test points, find first divergence point.
30% of boards failing ICT resistance check on R15. X-ray shows no solder issues. What next?
30% is systematic, not random. Check: (1) Is R15 correct value? Measure actual resistance on failed boards — if wrong value, wrong-part issue from a reel change. (2) ICT fixture probe making good contact? Worn probe gives false failures. (3) Is there a parallel resistance path in the circuit affecting the reading?
Product passes all your tests but fails at customer's site. What do you investigate?
Environment is the variable. Ask: ambient temp/humidity/altitude? Other EMI sources nearby? Their power supply quality? Grounding situation? Try to replicate in lab: temperature chamber, variable supply, nearby RF sources. Common culprits: temperature extremes, input power spikes, EMI from industrial equipment, humidity causing leakage currents.

Calculation Questions — Think Out Loud PRACTICE THESE

▶
LED needs 20mA, forward voltage 2V, powered from 5V. What resistor?
V across R = 5 − 2 = 3V. R = 3 / 0.02 = 150Ω. Power = 0.02² × 150 = 0.06W — standard ¼W resistor fine.
LDO: Vin=5V, Vout=3.3V, I=500mA. How much heat?
P_waste = (5 − 3.3) × 0.5 = 1.7 × 0.5 = 0.85W heat in LDO. Efficiency = 3.3/5 = 66% only. That's why buck converters are used instead at higher currents.
Signal period = 2ms. What is frequency?
f = 1/T = 1/0.002 = 500Hz.
12-bit ADC, Vref = 3.3V. What voltage change can it detect?
2¹² = 4096 levels. Resolution = 3.3 / 4096 ≈ 0.8mV per step.
FMEA: Severity=8, Occurrence=4, Detection=3. RPN and risk level?
RPN = 8 × 4 × 3 = 96. Max possible = 1000. Moderate risk — needs attention especially because Severity=8 means serious consequences if it occurs. Prioritise improving occurrence or detection.
200 boards tested, 174 passed. Yield? How many to analyse for Pareto?
Yield = 174/200 × 100 = 87%. Failed = 26. With only 26, analyse all of them — categorise each failure mode. Small enough for 100% analysis, which gives the true distribution for your Pareto.
