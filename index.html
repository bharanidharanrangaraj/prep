01 DMM — Digital Multimeter TOPIC 1 — STARTS HERE ▶
All Modes — What Each Does
  • DC Voltage (V—) — measure power rails, logic levels, battery voltage. Red probe to +, black to GND. Read voltage of red probe relative to black
  • AC Voltage (V~) — measure mains supply (230V India), transformer output. Never use on DC circuits
  • Resistance (Ω) — POWER OFF CIRCUIT FIRST. Measure component values, trace resistance. Live circuit = wrong reading
  • Continuity (beep )))) — beeps if resistance <~30Ω. For: solder joint check, trace check, short detection. Beep = connected. Silence = open
  • Diode Test — shows forward voltage drop. Silicon = 0.6–0.7V forward. OL (open loop) in reverse = good diode. 0V both ways = shorted. OL both ways = open
  • DC Current (A) — BREAK the circuit, insert DMM IN SERIES. Use current port on DMM (not voltage port — blows fuse or destroys DMM)
Dead Board — Step by Step with DMM
  • Step 1 — measure input voltage at power connector. Correct voltage arriving? (e.g., 5V ±5%)
  • Step 2 — measure LDO or buck converter output. Rail at correct voltage?
  • Step 3 — measure VCC at the main IC pin directly. Not just at regulator — trace may be broken
  • Step 4 — check ground continuity from supply to board GND pin
  • Step 5 — if all rails correct but device dead → check enable pins, reset line, communication lines
Important Limitations
  • DMM shows average steady-state voltage only — misses transients, noise, ripple
  • DMM has 10MΩ input impedance — barely loads the circuit. Pin may show 3.3V on DMM but collapse under real load
  • Continuity beep between VCC and GND is not always a short — could be LED, resistor, protection diode. Switch to resistance mode and read the actual value. True short = near 0Ω + component getting hot
Q: How do you use a DMM to find a short circuit?
  • Power OFF the circuit
  • Set DMM to continuity or resistance mode
  • Probe between VCC and GND — if beep, measure resistance value
  • Near 0Ω = likely short. Check schematic for any legitimate parallel paths first
  • Systematically isolate sections — disconnect sub-circuits one at a time until short disappears. That section contains the fault
  • Power ON with current-limited supply — shorted component gets warm. Find it with touch or thermal camera
Q: How do you measure current without breaking the circuit?
Use a clamp meter — it measures the magnetic field around a conductor without contact. Useful for high currents. For low currents on PCB, a shunt resistor (low value, e.g., 0.1Ω) in series — measure voltage across it, calculate I = V/R.
02 Failure Analysis (FA) TOPIC 2 — YOU BLANKED LAST TIME ▶
⚠ You forgot this in Round 1. Read these 6 steps out loud right now before the interview.
FA Flow — 6 Steps in Order
  • 1. Visual Inspection — naked eye → magnifier → microscope. Photos before touching anything. Look: burnt marks, swollen caps, solder bridges, cold joints, missing/wrong components, reversed polarity
  • 2. Electrical Characterisation — DMM + scope. Reproduce the failure. Measure all rails and key signals vs expected values
  • 3. Fault Isolation — use working unit as reference. Measure same points on both. First divergence = fault location
  • 4. Physical Analysis — X-ray (non-destructive, sees under BGA), cross-section (destructive, internal joint structure), SEM + EDS if needed
  • 5. Root Cause Identification — classify: Design / Material / Process / ESD / Workmanship
  • 6. Corrective Action — 8D report, FMEA update, ECN if design change needed
FA Tools
X-RAY
  • Non-destructive
  • Views solder under BGA/QFN
  • Detects: voids, bridges, missing balls
  • Standard for hidden solder joints
CROSS-SECTION
  • Destructive — cut + polish
  • Views internal solder structure
  • Shows: cracks, voids, intermetallic layer
  • Confirms thermal fatigue failures
SEM
  • Up to 100,000× magnification
  • Surface morphology, fracture details
  • Sees cracks invisible optically
  • Needs vacuum chamber
EDS (with SEM)
  • Identifies chemical elements
  • Confirms contamination, wrong alloy
  • Checks if solder is correct (SAC305)
  • Detects corrosion products
Common Failure Modes
  • Cold solder joint — dull, grey, grainy (not shiny). High resistance. Intermittent. Reflow with fresh solder
  • Solder bridge — blob connecting adjacent pins. Short circuit. Remove with wick
  • Open trace — broken PCB track. Continuity = open. Bridge with bodge wire
  • ESD damage — IC appears fine physically but dead or intermittent. No visual sign. Replace IC
  • Reversed polarity — electrolytic cap or diode backwards. Overheating, bulging, failure
  • Wrong component — same package, wrong value. Measure actual value vs BOM
  • Lifted pad — pad separated from PCB, looks soldered but no electrical connection
  • Tombstone — one end of SMD component lifts off pad during reflow
8D + FMEA — Quick Summary
  • D1 Team → D2 Describe problem → D3 Containment (TEMPORARY) → D4 Root cause (5-Why/Fishbone) → D5 Corrective action (PERMANENT) → D6 Implement → D7 Prevent recurrence → D8 Close
  • Containment ≠ Corrective action — containment stops the bleeding NOW. Corrective action is the permanent fix
RPN = Severity × Occurrence × Detection (max 1000)
  • Higher RPN = higher priority. Reduce by improving Detection or reducing Occurrence
  • Severity 9–10 = safety/regulatory failure — review even at low RPN
Q: What is the difference between ICT and FCT?
  • ICT (In-Circuit Test) — tests PCBA before product assembly. Bed-of-nails fixture contacts all test points. Checks: component values, opens, shorts. Catches assembly defects. Fast and cheap
  • FCT (Functional Test) — tests fully assembled product under real operating conditions. Exercises all functions: power on, comms, sensors, display. Catches functional and firmware integration failures
  • ICT = assembly quality. FCT = does the product actually work
03 LCR Meter TOPIC 3 — KNOW THIS WELL ▶
What Is an LCR Meter?
  • Measures L (Inductance — Henry, H), C (Capacitance — Farad, F), R (Resistance — Ohm, Ω)
  • More accurate than DMM for component measurement — especially for capacitors and inductors
  • Applies a known AC signal at a specific frequency and measures the component's impedance response
  • DMM can only measure resistance — LCR meter measures all three passive component types
How It Works
  • Applies AC test signal (e.g., 1kHz, 10kHz, 100kHz) across the component
  • Measures impedance (Z), phase angle, and calculates L, C, or R from the result
  • Capacitor impedance: Xc = 1/(2πfC) — at known frequency, measure Xc, calculate C
  • Inductor impedance: XL = 2πfL — at known frequency, measure XL, calculate L
  • Test frequency matters — measure capacitors at the frequency they'll be used in circuit
Key Parameters It Measures
  • Capacitance (C) — actual capacitance value in pF, nF, µF
  • ESR (Equivalent Series Resistance) — internal resistance of capacitor. High ESR = bad cap. Critical for electrolytic caps — high ESR causes heat, ripple, failure
  • Inductance (L) — actual inductance value in µH, mH, H
  • Q factor — quality factor. Higher Q = lower loss in inductor. Q = XL/R
  • D (Dissipation factor) — loss in capacitor. D = ESR × 2πfC. Low D = good cap
When You Use LCR Meter in FA
  • Verify component value matches BOM — wrong capacitor value is common NPI failure
  • Check electrolytic cap health — high ESR indicates degraded or failing cap even if capacitance looks ok
  • Verify inductors in switching power supplies — wrong inductance changes converter behaviour
  • Check crystal/resonator loading capacitors — wrong value shifts oscillation frequency
  • Incoming component quality inspection — verify batch components are within spec
Q: A board has a power supply ripple issue. How does LCR meter help?
  • Measure ESR of the output filter electrolytic capacitor
  • High ESR means the cap cannot filter ripple effectively — even if capacitance value is correct
  • ESR increases with age, temperature, and if cap was stressed beyond its voltage rating
  • Replace the cap — measure ESR of the new one to confirm it's low before installing
  • Also verify capacitance value — a degraded cap may have lost capacitance too
Q: Difference between LCR meter and DMM for component testing?
  • DMM resistance mode applies DC — only measures R. Cannot measure C or L
  • LCR meter applies AC at specific frequency — measures all three (L, C, R) plus derived parameters (ESR, Q, D)
  • LCR meter is far more accurate for capacitors — DMM may give rough capacitance in some modes but not ESR
  • For FA work: use DMM for quick voltage and continuity. Use LCR when you need to verify a specific passive component is within spec
Xc = 1/(2πfC)  |  XL = 2πfL  |  Z = √(R² + X²)
04 Oscilloscope TOPIC 4 — VERY LIKELY DEEP QUESTIONS ▶
Key Controls
  • Timebase (X axis — time/div) — how much time per grid square. Set so 1–2 full cycles fill the screen. 1kHz signal (1ms period) → use 0.2ms/div
  • Voltage scale (Y axis — V/div) — how much voltage per grid square. Signal should fill ~60% of screen height. If clipped → increase V/div. If tiny line → decrease V/div
  • Trigger — stabilises waveform. Rising edge trigger at ~50% of signal amplitude. Without trigger, waveform scrolls unreadably. Single trigger = capture one-time events
  • Probe ground clip — always connect to circuit GND first before probing any signal
  • ×10 probe — reduces loading on circuit (10× less current). Attenuates signal by 10× — set scope to compensate. Use ×10 for almost all work
  • Probe compensation — adjust trim cap until built-in 1kHz square wave has sharp flat corners. Do this before any measurement
Scope vs DMM — When to Use Each
  • DMM — steady-state DC voltage, resistance, continuity, slow measurements
  • Scope — anything that changes with time: ripple, noise, transients, clock signals, data lines, glitches, PWM, spikes that cause resets
  • If DMM shows correct voltage but circuit misbehaves → grab the scope
What to Look For in FA
  • Power rail ripple/noise — DMM shows 3.3V but scope shows 50mV switching noise at 500kHz. Causes ADC errors, communication failures, erratic MCU behaviour
  • Clock signal — correct frequency? Duty cycle ~50%? Clean edges or slow/ringy edges? Ringing = impedance mismatch, add series termination resistor
  • Voltage droop under load — rail drops when device draws current. Scope captures it, DMM misses it. Common cause of random resets
  • Glitches — short spikes that reset or corrupt data. Use single trigger to capture. Very hard to find without scope
  • Communication signals — is UART TX active? I2C SCL running? SPI CS toggling?
  • PWM — verify frequency and duty cycle are correct
Reading a Waveform — Say This in Interview
  • First state: "Timebase is X ms/div, so period is approximately Y ms"
  • Then: "Frequency = 1/period = Z Hz"
  • Then: "Amplitude is approximately A volts peak-to-peak"
  • Then describe what you see: clean square wave / noisy / ringy edges / clipped / unexpected dips
  • Then interpret: "This could indicate..."
Q: Board resets randomly. How do you use scope to find the cause?
  • Connect scope channel 1 to the RESET pin — trigger on reset going active (LOW)
  • Connect channel 2 to the main power rail (3.3V or VCC)
  • Set trigger on channel 1 falling edge — scope waits for the reset event
  • When reset occurs, scope captures what happened JUST BEFORE
  • Look at channel 2: did the power rail dip before the reset? If yes → power supply issue, not firmware
  • If power rail is clean during reset → watchdog timer firing (firmware hung), or external reset signal being asserted
Q: What is ringing on a scope and what causes it?
  • Ringing = oscillations (bouncing waveform) at the edge of a square wave — signal overshoots then undershoots before settling
  • Caused by: impedance mismatch between driver and trace (transmission line effect), trace inductance, capacitive loading
  • Fix: add a series termination resistor (22–100Ω) at the driver output. Reduces reflection and damps the ringing
  • Problem: ringing can cause logic to read the wrong state if it crosses the threshold voltage
05 Power Supply TOPIC 5 ▶
Power Supply Types
  • LDO (Linear Dropout Regulator) — simple, burns excess as heat. Efficiency = Vout/Vin
  • 5V→3.3V at 500mA: heat = (5−3.3)×0.5 = 0.85W, efficiency = 66%
  • Buck converter — switching, steps DOWN efficiently 85–95%. Vout = Vin × duty cycle
  • Boost converter — switching, steps UP. 3.7V battery → 5V USB output
  • LDO: use for low current, noise-sensitive analog circuits. Buck: use when current is high or voltage difference is large
Lab Power Supply (Bench PSU)
  • Set output voltage to your required value before connecting to circuit
  • Current limiting — set current limit to just above expected draw. If circuit draws more (short circuit), PSU limits current instead of burning components. Essential for debugging
  • Start with low voltage, power on, observe current. Normal current = correct. High current = short somewhere
  • Two-channel PSU: use one channel for positive supply, one for negative (for op-amp circuits)
  • Remote sensing — compensates for voltage drop in long cables by measuring voltage at the load, not at PSU terminals
Power Failures — How to Debug
  • Rail at 0V — fuse blown? LDO enable pin not asserted? Short circuit pulling rail down? Input voltage missing?
  • Rail low (e.g., 2.8V instead of 3.3V) — overloaded (too much current draw)? LDO/buck under-spec? Long trace resistance causing drop?
  • Rail noisy — check with scope. Insufficient decoupling caps? Switching regulator layout issue? Ground plane broken?
  • Rail correct at PSU but low at IC pin — trace resistance or connector resistance causing drop under load. Measure at IC pin directly
  • LDO hot — calculate: P = (Vin−Vout)×I. Check if within thermal limit of package. Add heatsink or switch to buck
Ohm's Law & Power — Quick Reference
V = IR  |  P = VI  |  P = I²R  |  P = V²/R
Series R: Rtotal = R1+R2  |  Parallel R: R = (R1×R2)/(R1+R2)
Q: How do you use a bench PSU to debug a new board for the first time?
  • Set PSU to required voltage (e.g., 5V). Set current limit low — just above expected normal draw (e.g., 200mA for a small MCU board)
  • Connect to board. Power on. Watch current meter on PSU
  • Normal: current rises to expected level, stays stable. Board works
  • High current immediately: short circuit somewhere — PSU limits, nothing burns. Investigate short
  • Current gradually increasing: thermal runaway or a component charging slowly
  • Zero current: fuse blown, connector issue, or board not actually connected properly
  • After confirming no short, increase current limit to operating maximum and proceed with testing
06 Communication Protocols — SPI, I2C, UART & More TOPIC 6 — EXPECT DEEP QUESTIONS ▶
UART
  • Wires: TX (transmit) + RX (receive) + GND. No clock — asynchronous
  • Both sides must be configured to same baud rate (bits/second). Common: 9600, 115200
  • Frame: Start bit (LOW) → 8 data bits (LSB first) → Stop bit (HIGH). Idle = HIGH
  • TX of device A connects to RX of device B (and vice versa). TX→TX = nothing works
  • Failures: baud rate mismatch (garbled output), TX/RX swapped, voltage level mismatch (5V TX→3.3V RX), line floating (no pull-up)
I2C
  • Wires: SDA (data) + SCL (clock). Only 2 wires for unlimited devices. Each device has 7-bit address
  • Open-drain — devices can only pull LOW, never drive HIGH. Pull-up resistors MANDATORY (4.7kΩ standard, 2.2kΩ fast mode)
  • Master generates clock, initiates all transactions. Slave responds only when addressed
  • Transaction: START → address + R/W → ACK from slave → data → ACK → STOP
  • ACK bit — slave pulls SDA LOW during 9th clock pulse to confirm it received the byte. No ACK = device not present, wrong address, or device busy
  • Failures: missing pull-ups (SDA/SCL stuck LOW or floating), wrong device address, bus lockup (slave stuck holding SDA LOW — fix: clock SCL 9 times then STOP), address conflict, 5V device on 3.3V bus
SPI
  • Wires: MOSI + MISO + SCLK + CS (one CS per slave device)
  • Synchronous, full-duplex (send and receive simultaneously). Faster than I2C (up to tens of MHz)
  • CS pin — master pulls CS LOW to select device. Only one CS active at a time. Releases HIGH to deselect
  • SPI Modes (CPOL/CPHA) — 4 modes define when data is sampled vs when clock transitions. Mode 0 most common (clock idles LOW, data sampled on rising edge). Wrong mode = shifted/corrupted data
  • Failures: CS not going LOW before clock, wrong CPOL/CPHA mode, clock too fast for device, MOSI/MISO swapped, CS floating HIGH
RS232 vs RS485
  • RS232 — single-ended (voltage vs GND). ±12V swing. Point-to-point only. Max ~15m. Common for instrument serial ports
  • RS485 — differential (A and B wires). Noise immune. Multi-drop (up to 32 devices). Up to 1200m. Used for Modbus RTU (my PZEM library), industrial equipment, production line control
USB (CDC)
  • Differential pair (D+ and D−) + VBUS (5V) + GND
  • USB CDC (Communication Device Class) — makes device appear as virtual serial port on computer. Used by Arduino, ESP32 for programming and debug
  • Enumeration: host requests descriptor from device. Descriptor identifies device type and capabilities. Failure = "Unknown Device" in Device Manager
  • Failures: D+/D− swapped, wrong descriptor data, missing pull-up on D+
Q: I2C device is not responding. How do you debug?
  • Check pull-up resistors on SDA and SCL — are they present and the right value?
  • Use logic analyser or scope on SCL — is the clock running when master initiates transaction?
  • Check SDA line — is the master sending the correct device address? Is the slave pulling SDA LOW for the ACK bit?
  • Verify device address — check datasheet, check address selection pin (some ICs have ADDR pin pulled HIGH or LOW)
  • Check device power supply — VCC at the correct voltage at the IC pin?
  • Check for bus lockup — is SDA stuck LOW? Clock 9 times on SCL then send STOP to release
  • Verify voltage levels — 5V device on 3.3V bus can cause issues
Q: SPI sensor not responding even though clock and CS look correct on scope.
  • Check SPI mode (CPOL/CPHA) — does firmware mode match sensor datasheet requirement?
  • Check MOSI — is the correct command byte actually being sent? Put logic analyser on MOSI and verify
  • Check clock frequency — is it within the sensor's maximum rated SPI speed?
  • Check CS timing — CS must go LOW BEFORE clock starts. Some sensors require a minimum setup time
  • Check sensor VCC — is it actually powered?
  • Check MISO — is the sensor driving MISO at all? If MISO = floating, sensor may not be outputting
Q: What is the difference between synchronous and asynchronous communication?
  • Synchronous — shared clock signal between sender and receiver. Receiver uses clock edge to sample data exactly. Examples: SPI, I2C. More reliable, higher speed possible
  • Asynchronous — no shared clock. Both sides use an agreed baud rate (internal clock). Sender marks start of transmission with a start bit. Examples: UART, RS232, RS485. Simpler wiring, works over longer distances
Protocol Comparison Table
UART: 2 wires, async, baud rate I2C: 2 wires, sync, addressed, pull-ups SPI: 4 wires, sync, fast, full-duplex RS485: differential, multi-drop, industrial USB: differential pair, high speed, enumeration

08 Scenario & Calculation Questions EXPECT THESE — THINK OUT LOUD ▶
LED: forward voltage 2V, current 20mA, supply 5V. Series resistor?
V across R = 5−2 = 3V. R = V/I = 3/0.02 = 150Ω. Power in R = 3×0.02 = 0.06W → standard ¼W resistor fine.
LDO: 5V in, 3.3V out, 500mA load. Heat dissipated? Efficiency?
Heat = (5−3.3) × 0.5 = 0.85W. Efficiency = 3.3/5 = 66%. At 1A: heat = 1.7W — needs heatsink.
Signal period = 2ms. Frequency? If duty cycle 30%, what is ON time?
f = 1/T = 1/0.002 = 500Hz. ON time = 0.30 × 2ms = 0.6ms.
12-bit ADC, Vref = 3.3V. Resolution per step?
2¹² = 4096 levels. Resolution = 3.3/4096 = 0.8mV per step.
FMEA: S=8, O=4, D=3. RPN?
RPN = 8×4×3 = 96. Moderate but Severity=8 is high — review even at moderate RPN.
200 boards tested, 174 passed. Yield?
Yield = 174/200 × 100 = 87%. Failed = 26. Analyse all 26 for Pareto of failure modes.
Two boards same batch — one works, one doesn't. How do you approach?
Design is probably fine. Use working board as reference. Measure same test points on both. First measurement that differs = fault location. Then isolate to component level in that area.
Board works fine at room temp, fails after 30 minutes.
Thermal failure. Use thermal camera to find hot component. Suspects: electrolytic cap (ESR increases with heat), LDO in thermal shutdown (calculate P = (Vin−Vout)×I, check thermal rating), solder joint opening with thermal expansion.
Capacitor fully charged in DC circuit — current flowing?
Zero. Fully charged cap in DC = open circuit. Current only flows while voltage is changing.
You replaced a component and board works. Are you done?
No. Fix the root cause — WHY did it fail? Wrong rating? Reversed? Counterfeit? That answer determines if other boards in batch are at risk. Document and initiate corrective action.
